Regulatory

DSA 2021 Compliance & CDSE Reports

Enquire →

India's Dam Safety Act 2021 has fundamentally changed the obligations of dam owners and State Dam Safety Organisations. Compliance is no longer optional — and the regulatory framework is detailed, technical, and still being operationalised. This is where institutional experience matters most.

Having spent three years at NDSA (2023–2026) directly involved in formulating regulations, developing the DHARMA portal inspection questionnaire, and interacting with dam owners and SDSOs across India, the compliance support offered here goes beyond reading the Act. It draws on direct knowledge of how NDSA interprets its own regulations and what it expects from dam owners in practice.

Key Regulatory Milestone
Under DSA 2021, every "specified dam" — height ≥ 15 m, or height 10–15 m with qualifying conditions — must constitute a Dam Safety Unit, appoint a Dam Safety Officer, undergo annual and special inspections, maintain an O&M panel, and prepare a Comprehensive Dam Safety Evaluation (CDSE) report. Non-compliance carries legal consequences for dam owners.

Emergency Preparedness

Dam Break Analysis & EAP Preparation

Enquire →

An Emergency Action Plan is not a document — it is a tested, actionable response system that must function under the worst possible conditions. Preparing one correctly requires both technical rigour and an understanding of the downstream reality the dam faces.

EAP preparation under the NDSA framework (2024 Hazard Classification Regulations) is now a precisely defined process. The dam's hazard class — H1 through H6 under the PCI-based system — determines the EAP tier, the warning time requirements, and the notification chain. Dam break analysis feeds the inundation maps that underpin the entire plan. Each component must be technically defensible and practically implementable by the dam owner.

NDSA 2024 Hazard Classification
The NDSA Vulnerability and Hazard Classification Regulations 2024 use a Potential Consequences Index (PCI) — combining Population at Risk, dam height, project benefits, critical arrival time, property damage, and environmental-cultural factors — to assign dams to hazard classes H1 through H6. The hazard class directly determines EAP requirements and the level of emergency response preparation needed.
What This Service Covers
  • Hazard classification under NDSA 2024 Regulations — PCI computation, H1–H6 assignment
  • Dam break analysis — breach parameter estimation, peak outflow, routing
  • Inundation mapping — flood wave travel time, affected areas, depth contours
  • Population at Risk (PAR) determination — census-based, field-verified
  • EAP document preparation — NDSA-approved template, all required sections
  • Warning and notification system design — triggers, thresholds, communication chain
  • Tabletop exercise design for dam owner staff
  • EAP review and update for existing plans

Hydrology

IDF Review & PMF Studies

Enquire →

Hydrological adequacy is at the heart of dam safety. A dam designed to a flood standard that is now demonstrably inadequate — due to revised PMF estimates, updated CWC data, or changed catchment conditions — carries real residual risk regardless of its structural condition.

Under DSA 2021, every specified dam must be evaluated against PMF — there is no IS 11223 size-based relaxation to SPF for regulatory compliance purposes. This creates a significant workload for dam owners, many of whom have ageing designs based on outdated hydrological data. The IDF review service addresses this systematically — evaluating existing design floods, comparing against current PMF estimates, and where deficiencies exist, developing a technically defensible remediation pathway under the ALARP framework.

The PMF Position Under DSA 2021
NDSA's position is unambiguous: every specified dam requires PMF evaluation. The IS 11223:1985 size-based gradation (large → PMF, intermediate → SPF, small → 100-year) applies to design of new structures — it does not constitute a relaxation of the safety review standard under DSA 2021. Where full structural accommodation of PMF is not immediately feasible, a risk-informed ALARP argument with documented remediation measures is the accepted pathway.
What This Service Covers
  • Review of existing design flood — adequacy assessment against current standards
  • PMF estimation — CWC Hydrometeorological method, unit hydrograph approach
  • SPF computation — regional flood frequency analysis, index flood method
  • Stage-discharge data quality review — Cd consistency, drowning correction
  • IDF benchmark determination per IS 11223 and CWC Manual guidance
  • Flood routing through reservoir and spillway — adequacy of spillway capacity
  • Barrage-specific IDF assessment — CWC Manual for barrages
  • ALARP-framed deficiency report with remediation options
Himalayan River Specialism
Particular expertise in Himalayan river hydrology — where PMF estimates are consistently being revised upward, catchment response is rapid, sediment and boulder loads are extreme, and standard stage-discharge relationships require special treatment. Projects include Joshiyara Barrage (Bhagirathi), Tapovan Vishnugad (Dhauliganga), and Koteshwar HEP (Bhagirathi).

Design & Review

Hydraulic Structure Design & Review

Enquire →

Hydraulic and structural design of barrages, spillways, stilling basins, and canal systems — developed over nearly four decades of CWC design directorate work, covering projects from Rajghat to Tapovan Vishnugad to Par-Tapi-Narmada.

Design reviews for existing structures often reveal problems that were not apparent at original construction — Froude numbers outside the intended range for the stilling basin type, tailwater deficits, insufficient basin length, or appurtenances unsuitable for sediment-heavy flows. Systematic hydraulic review identifies these issues and proposes retrofits that are both technically sound and practical within the constraints of the existing structure.

Risk Assessment Framework — Three Tiers
The CWC/DRIP framework operates as a progressive funnel: Tier 1 (RRSSD rapid screening — all specified dams), Tier 2 (SQRA semi-quantitative risk assessment — high-scoring dams), Tier 3 (QRA full quantitative risk assessment — critical failure modes). The screening score and sub-score pattern determines which tier each dam requires — and which specific failure modes drive the risk. This service covers all three tiers.
