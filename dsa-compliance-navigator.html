Chapter II & Section 30 — Institutional Framework
01
Dam Safety Unit, DSO & O&M Panel
Section 30 · DSA Rules 2023 · NDSA Advisory 2022
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Most dam safety failures in India have occurred not because engineers didn't know what to do — but because no one was institutionally responsible for doing it. Gate malfunction at Machhu, deferred maintenance decisions at multiple sites — the common thread is diffused or absent accountability. Section 30 mandates a dedicated Dam Safety Unit so that safety is someone's primary job, not the third item on a Chief Engineer's agenda. The DSO appointment creates a named, qualified individual who is legally responsible.
What the law requires
  • Constitute a Dam Safety Unit (DSU) for every specified dam — Section 30
  • Appoint a qualified Dam Safety Officer (DSO) — must meet prescribed qualifications under DSA Rules
  • Constitute an O&M Panel of experts covering structural, hydraulic, geotechnical, and electro-mechanical aspects
  • Designate clear roles, responsibilities, and reporting lines for dam safety management
  • DSU constitution and DSO appointment reported to SDSO and recorded on DHARMA portal

Chapter IV — Dam Safety Measures & Inspections
02
Mandatory Inspections — Pre & Post Monsoon
Chapter IV · NDSA Circular 1/2022 · TG-02/2022 · DHARMA
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
India's dam incident history shows that many failures were preceded by visible warning signs — seepage, cracking, gate malfunction — that were either not observed or not acted upon. The pre-monsoon inspection ensures the dam is structurally and operationally ready before maximum hydraulic loading. The post-monsoon inspection captures damage caused by the flood season before it advances unseen through winter. Both must be formally recorded and uploaded — undocumented inspections, even if conducted, provide no institutional protection and no regulatory audit trail.
What the law requires
  • Pre-monsoon inspection completed by 30th June each year — NDSA Circular 1/2022
  • Post-monsoon inspection completed by 30th November each year
  • Inspection using prescribed checklist — visual, structural, mechanical, instrumentation, reservoir preparedness
  • Inspection report submitted to SDSO within 15 days of inspection
  • Report uploaded to DHARMA portal — mandatory digital record
  • Special inspections after extreme events — floods exceeding 70% of design flood, earthquakes, unusual observations
  • Any distress condition reported immediately to SDSO — no deferral permitted

Chapter III — Obligations of Dam Owners: Technical Reviews
03
Inflow Design Flood (IDF) Review
Section 13 · IS 11223:1985 · CWC IDF Guidelines
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
A dam may look structurally sound yet be hydraulically unsafe — because the Inflow Design Flood it was designed for is now inadequate given updated PMF estimates, changed catchment conditions, or climate-driven shifts in extreme rainfall. Many dams in India were designed to older CWC standards where PMF estimates were significantly lower than what updated hydrometeorological analysis now yields — particularly in Himalayan catchments. An inadequate spillway that cannot pass the PMF is an overtopping risk regardless of structural condition. The IDF review is the mechanism to detect and address this hidden risk before a major flood event exposes it.
What the law requires
  • Review IDF against current Probable Maximum Flood (PMF) estimates — Section 13
  • High-hazard dams: review every 5–10 years or after a major flood event
  • Medium-hazard dams: every 10–15 years or after significant watershed changes
  • Review must account for updated CWC hydrometeorological data, changed land use, and climate trends
  • Spillway adequacy assessed against current IDF — freeboard check, gate capacity, energy dissipation
  • IDF review report submitted to SDSO; uploaded to DHARMA portal
  • Where IDF deficiency is identified — remediation pathway under ALARP framework with NDSA/SDSO

04
Seismic Hazard Assessment
Section 14 · IS 1893 · BIS Seismic Zone Map
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Many existing dams in India were designed using seismic coefficients that are now demonstrably inadequate — because they predate the current IS 1893 seismic zone map, or were designed to Zone II standards in areas now reclassified as Zone IV or V. An embankment dam in a high seismic zone, designed without proper dynamic analysis, carries a liquefaction or slope stability risk under Maximum Credible Earthquake loading that no annual inspection can detect. The seismic hazard assessment is the one technical review that can only be done analytically — the risk is invisible until the earthquake arrives. Reservoir-Induced Seismicity (RIS) adds a further dimension for large reservoirs.
What the law requires
  • Conduct seismic hazard assessment against Maximum Credible Earthquake (MCE) for the dam's region — Section 14
  • Seismotectonic study — identify active fault zones, historical seismicity, ground motion parameters
  • Structural response analysis — dynamic analysis of dam body under seismic loading (FEA where warranted)
  • Assessment of Reservoir-Induced Seismicity (RIS) for large reservoirs
  • Accelerographs / strong motion sensors installed and functional at dam site
  • High-hazard dams: assessment every 5–10 years or after major seismic event (≥ Operational Basis Earthquake level)
  • Report submitted to SDSO; uploaded to DHARMA

05
Instrumentation — Installation & Monitoring
Section 15 · NDSA Technical Guidelines · IS Codes
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
A dam without functioning instrumentation is operating blind. Seepage developing in the foundation, pore pressure building in the embankment, slow settlement or lateral displacement — these distress conditions develop invisibly and reveal themselves visually only when they are already at an advanced and often irreversible stage. Instrumentation is the early warning system for the dam's internal health. The particular challenge for existing dams is that many critical instruments — piezometers, strain gauges — were either never installed, or have degraded beyond use, and physical retrofitting into the core or foundation is now impossible. This makes a functioning surface and gallery instrumentation system even more critical.
What the law requires
  • Install and maintain appropriate instrumentation for the dam type and hazard level — Section 15
  • Minimum: piezometers (uplift, pore pressure), seepage measurement (V-notch weirs), settlement gauges, inclinometers where applicable
  • For high-hazard dams: accelerographs / strong motion sensors mandatory
  • Instrumentation data recorded at prescribed frequency; reviewed by DSO and O&M Panel
  • Trend analysis — patterns correlated with reservoir level, rainfall, and seismic events
  • Action levels (alert/alarm/emergency thresholds) defined and integrated with EAP
  • Non-functional instruments identified, reported, and replacement planned
  • Data uploaded to DHARMA portal as part of inspection records

Chapter IV — Comprehensive Safety Evaluation
06
Comprehensive Dam Safety Evaluation (CDSE)
NDSA Regulation 12/2022 · Every 5 Years · External Panel
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Annual inspections catch visible deterioration. The CDSE catches what annual inspections miss — systematic structural deficiencies, design-standard inadequacies, failure mode vulnerabilities, and cumulative drift from safe operating parameters that develops slowly over years. The 5-year cycle is modelled on international dam safety practice (USSD, ANCOLD, ICOLD) where periodic comprehensive review by an independent expert panel has consistently identified dam safety risks that in-house inspections did not. For dams built before 1980 — designed to standards that predate modern PMF methods, seismic codes, and geotechnical understanding — the CDSE often reveals the most significant safety concerns.
What the law requires
  • CDSE every 5 years for all specified dams — NDSA Regulation 12/2022; also triggered after extreme events
  • Covers: structural stability, hydraulic adequacy, geotechnical performance, instrumentation adequacy, seismic vulnerability, EAP effectiveness
  • External expert panel mandatory for large/high-hazard dams — Regulation 13/2022
  • CDSE report submitted to NDSA and SDSO; uploaded to DHARMA within 30 days of completion
  • Recommendations implemented within 12 months; SDSO and NDSA monitor progress
  • Non-compliance can result in suspension of dam operations under Section 38

Chapter V — Emergency Action Plan & Risk Reduction
07
Emergency Action Plan (EAP)
Section 26 · NDSA Reg. 8–11/2022 · NDSA Hazard Classification 2024
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
The 1979 Machhu Dam disaster killed an estimated 10,000–25,000 people — not primarily because the dam failed, but because there was no warning system, no evacuation plan, and no downstream authority knew a disaster was imminent until the flood wave arrived. The EAP mandate exists to ensure that the consequences of dam failure, when it does occur, are managed through pre-planned and tested response rather than improvised chaos. An EAP is not a document — it is a rehearsed system that must function under the worst possible conditions, often in the dark, during a flood, with communication systems partially down.
What the law requires
  • Hazard class determination — PCI-based H1–H6 classification under NDSA Hazard & Vulnerability Classification Regulations 2024
  • Dam break analysis — breach parameter estimation, peak outflow, flood routing, inundation mapping
  • EAP document — inundation maps, notification flowcharts, evacuation zones, roles & responsibilities, emergency communication — Regulation 8/2022
  • SDSO approval of EAP — Regulation 9/2022
  • Annual mock drill — tabletop exercise or field drill; results uploaded to DHARMA
  • Community awareness programme — signage, local-language alerts, sensitisation — Regulation 10/2022
  • EAP updated every 5 years or after major changes, incidents, or downstream population changes
  • Integration with District Disaster Management Plan (DDMP)

Chapter VII — Documentation, O&M & DHARMA Compliance
08
Documentation, O&M Manual & DHARMA Portal
Section 2(j), 2(r), 2(g) · NDSA Reg. 14/2022 · DHARMA
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
When NDSA inspectors arrive at a dam site and ask for the original design memorandum, the as-built drawings, the geological investigation report, or the gate operation history — and these documents don't exist, or can't be located, or were lost in an office transfer — the dam owner is both legally exposed and operationally blind. Documentation is the institutional memory of a dam. Without it, every safety review and every emergency decision starts from zero. The DHARMA mandate converts this institutional memory into a national digital database — so that no dam's safety history is lost when personnel change or physical records are destroyed.
What the law requires
  • O&M Manual — written procedures for normal operations, gate operations, and emergency response — Section 2(r)
  • Log Books — day-to-day records of operations, observations, maintenance, and incidents
  • Documentation file — design memorandum, as-built drawings, geological reports, quality control records, inspection history, CDSE reports — Section 2(j)
  • Dam registered and active on DHARMA portal — inspection questionnaire current; all required reports uploaded within prescribed timelines
  • Incident reporting — any dam incident as defined under Section 2(g) reported to SDSO immediately; no unreported incidents
  • Records of all alterations, enlargements, or repairs that may affect dam safety — Section 2(k)
