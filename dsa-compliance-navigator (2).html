Chapter II / Section 30 — Institutional Setup
01
Dam Safety Unit (DSU) & Dam Safety Officer (DSO)
Section 30 · DSA Rules 2023
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Most dam safety failures in India have occurred not because engineers did not know what to do — but because no one was institutionally responsible for doing it. Diffused accountability, where dam safety is the third item on a Chief Engineer's agenda, is a systemic failure mode. Section 30 mandates a dedicated Dam Safety Unit so safety is someone's primary job. The DSO appointment creates a named, qualified individual who is legally accountable.
What the law requires
  • Constitute a Dam Safety Unit (DSU) for every specified dam
  • Appoint a qualified Dam Safety Officer (DSO) meeting DSA Rules 2023 qualifications
  • DSU constitution and DSO appointment reported to SDSO and recorded on DHARMA
  • DSO must have authority to act and is primary contact for SDSO/NDSA

~ Partial

Why this clause exists
A dam's safety involves multiple engineering disciplines — structural, hydraulic, geotechnical, electromechanical — that rarely coexist in a single dam owner's in-house team. The O&M Panel brings together the expert disciplines needed for holistic technical oversight. Without it, critical decisions are made by generalists or left unmade. The Panel also provides institutional continuity when personnel change.
What the law requires
  • Constitute O&M Panel covering structural, hydraulic, geotechnical, and electromechanical disciplines
  • Panel reviews instrumentation data and inspection findings periodically
  • Panel recommendations documented and acted upon — records maintained
  • Constitution reported to SDSO and recorded on DHARMA

Chapter III — Obligations of Dam Owners: Technical Reviews
03
Inflow Design Flood (IDF) Review — PMF & GLOF
Section 13 · IS 11223:1985 · CWC IDF Guidelines
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
A dam may look structurally sound yet be hydraulically unsafe — because the Inflow Design Flood it was designed for is now inadequate. Many Indian dams were designed to older CWC standards where PMF estimates were significantly lower than what updated hydrometeorological analysis now yields. An inadequate spillway is an overtopping risk regardless of structural condition. For Himalayan dams, Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOF) represent an additional extreme flood scenario absent from original design assumptions for most existing structures.
What the law requires
  • Review IDF against current Probable Maximum Flood (PMF) — Section 13
  • For Himalayan/glaciated catchments: GLOF scenario evaluated as part of IDF review
  • High-hazard dams: review every 5–10 years or after major flood
  • Spillway adequacy assessed against current IDF — freeboard, gate capacity, energy dissipation
  • Where deficiency found: ALARP remediation pathway documented with SDSO/NDSA
  • Report submitted to SDSO and uploaded to DHARMA

04
Seismic Hazard Assessment
Section 14 · IS 1893 · OBE/SEE Framework
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Many existing dams were designed using seismic coefficients now demonstrably inadequate — predating the current IS 1893 seismic zone map, or designed to Zone II standards in areas now classified Zone IV or V. An embankment dam in a high seismic zone, designed without proper dynamic analysis, carries a liquefaction or slope instability risk under Maximum Credible Earthquake loading that no visual inspection can detect. The seismic risk is invisible until the earthquake arrives.
What the law requires
  • Seismic hazard assessment against Maximum Credible Earthquake (MCE) — Section 14
  • Seismotectonic study — active faults, historical seismicity, ground motion parameters
  • Structural response analysis under OBE and SEE loading
  • Assessment of Reservoir-Induced Seismicity (RIS) for large reservoirs
  • High-hazard dams: every 5–10 years or after major seismic event
  • Report submitted to SDSO and uploaded to DHARMA

05
Instrumentation — Review & Recording
Section 15 · NDSA TG-02/2022
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
A dam without functioning instrumentation is operating blind. Seepage developing in the foundation, pore pressure building in the embankment, slow settlement — these distress conditions develop invisibly and reveal themselves visually only when already at an advanced and often irreversible stage. The key compliance question is not just whether instruments exist, but whether they have been reviewed by a competent person and findings formally recorded. Unread instruments are no better than absent ones.
What the law requires
  • Appropriate instrumentation installed for dam type and hazard level — Section 15
  • Key question: Has instrumentation been reviewed by a competent person and findings recorded?
  • Minimum: piezometers, seepage weirs, settlement gauges, inclinometers as applicable
  • Data read at prescribed frequency; trend analysis carried out; thresholds defined and linked to EAP
  • Non-functional instruments identified and replacement planned
  • Readings and review findings uploaded to DHARMA

Chapter IV — Dam Safety Measures
06
Mandatory Inspections — Pre & Post Monsoon
Chapter IV · NDSA Circular 1/2022 · DHARMA
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Many dam incidents in India were preceded by visible warning signs — seepage staining, cracking, gate malfunction — that were either not observed or not acted upon. The pre-monsoon inspection ensures the dam is ready before maximum hydraulic loading. The post-monsoon inspection captures damage from the flood season before it advances through winter. Formally recorded and uploaded inspections create the audit trail that protects both the dam owner and the downstream community.
What the law requires
  • Pre-monsoon inspection by 30th June each year — NDSA Circular 1/2022
  • Post-monsoon inspection by 30th November each year
  • Prescribed checklist — visual, structural, mechanical, instrumentation, reservoir preparedness
  • Report to SDSO within 15 days; uploaded to DHARMA portal
  • Any distress condition reported to SDSO immediately — no deferral

07
Comprehensive Dam Safety Evaluation (CDSE)
NDSA Regulation 12/2022 · Every 5 Years
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Annual inspections catch visible deterioration. The CDSE catches what annual inspections miss — systematic structural deficiencies, design-standard inadequacies, and failure mode vulnerabilities that develop slowly over years. The 5-year cycle follows international practice (USSD, ANCOLD, ICOLD) where independent expert review has consistently identified risks that in-house inspections did not. For dams built before 1980 the CDSE often surfaces the most significant safety concerns.
What the law requires
  • CDSE every 5 years — NDSA Regulation 12/2022; also after extreme events
  • Covers structural, hydraulic, geotechnical, seismic, instrumentation, and EAP aspects
  • External expert panel mandatory for large/high-hazard dams — Regulation 13/2022
  • Report to NDSA and SDSO; uploaded to DHARMA within 30 days
  • Recommendations implemented within 12 months

Chapter V — Emergency Action Plan, EWS & Risk
08
Emergency Action Plan (EAP)
Section 26 · NDSA Reg. 8–11/2022 · Hazard Classification 2024
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
The 1979 Machhu Dam disaster killed an estimated 10,000–25,000 people — not because engineering failed alone, but because there was no warning system, no evacuation plan, and no downstream authority knew a disaster was imminent until the flood wave arrived. The EAP mandate ensures consequences of dam failure are managed through pre-planned, tested response rather than improvised chaos. An EAP is not a document — it is a rehearsed system that must function in the dark, during a flood, with communication partially down.
What the law requires
  • Hazard class determination — PCI-based H1–H6 under NDSA Hazard Classification Regulations 2024
  • Dam break analysis — breach parameters, peak outflow, inundation mapping
  • EAP document — notification flowcharts, evacuation zones, roles, communication system
  • SDSO approval of EAP — Regulation 9/2022
  • Annual mock drill — tabletop or field; results uploaded to DHARMA
  • Community awareness — signage, local-language alerts; integration with District DMP
  • EAP updated every 5 years or after major changes/incidents

09
Early Warning System (EWS / DSEWS / EFWS)
Chapter V · Risk Reduction · EAP Integration
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
An EAP without a functioning Early Warning System is a plan that cannot be activated in time. EWS is the proactive, real-time component of dam safety — the sensing and alert layer that detects an impending emergency and initiates the EAP before failure occurs. DSA 2021 positions EWS as a non-structural risk reduction measure under Chapter V, distinct from structural instrumentation. A DSEWS monitors the dam's structural and hydrological status; an EFWS delivers downstream community alerts. Both must be operational and integrated with EAP activation levels.
What the law requires
  • DSEWS — hydro-meteorological network: rain gauges, reservoir level sensors, upstream gauges, telemetry
  • Structural distress thresholds linked to automated alerts to DSO
  • EFWS — downstream community warning: sirens, SMS, public address, coordination with SDMA/NDMA/district
  • EWS triggers formally defined and integrated with EAP activation levels
  • EWS tested periodically; test results recorded and uploaded to DHARMA
  • Institutional ownership clear — who operates the system and responds to alerts

Why this clause exists — and a critical clarification
Risk Assessment is the integrating framework of DSA 2021 — all other exercises (IDF review, seismic assessment, instrumentation, inspections, EAP) feed into the risk picture. Without RA, compliance becomes paperwork rather than safety. Critical point: RA under the DRIP manual is failure-mode-wise, not dam-wise. A single specified dam may have certain failure modes qualifying for SQRA (Tier 2) and others for QRA (Tier 3). The Tier 1 RRSSD screening surfaces which failure modes score heavily — those then receive appropriate deeper assessment. The dam is not classified as "Tier 2" — specific failure modes of that dam are.

Chapter VII / Section 2 — Documentation & Records
11
Log Books — Maintenance & Currency
Section 2(j) · Continuous Obligation
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Log books are the continuous institutional memory of a dam's day-to-day life. When a distress condition is investigated the log book is the first document examined. Gaps in log book records, or records that omit unusual observations, systematically destroy the information needed to detect developing failure modes. NDSA treats log book maintenance as a standalone monitoring item precisely because it is a continuous, daily obligation — not bundled with other documentation.
What the law requires
  • Log books maintained continuously — operations, gate operations, reservoir levels, instrumentation readings
  • All unusual observations recorded without exception — seepage changes, cracking, gate malfunction, flood events
  • Maintenance activities and repair works recorded with outcomes
  • DSO reviews log books periodically
  • Section 2(g) incident entries made and reported to SDSO

12
O&M Manual — Preparation, Revision & Currency
Section 2(r) · SDSO Approval Required
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
Many dams are operated by personnel who inherited the dam without inheriting the engineering knowledge behind its operation. Gate opening sequences, maximum release rates, flood operation protocols, emergency closure procedures — these must be written, approved, and kept current. An outdated O&M Manual is almost as dangerous as no manual — it may specify gate operation rules based on hydraulic conditions that have since changed. NDSA treats O&M Manual preparation and revision as a separate compliance item from technical documentation.
What the law requires
  • O&M Manual prepared — normal operation, gate operations, maintenance, emergency response — Section 2(r)
  • Manual approved by SDSO before use
  • Manual revised whenever significant changes occur — new gates, raised FRL, changed downstream conditions, revised EAP
  • All dam operating personnel trained on current Manual
  • Manual kept at dam site and available to SDSO/NDSA inspectors

13
Technical Documentation File
Section 2(j) · Design, Construction & Investigation Records
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
When a distress condition is identified, the first question is: what does the original design say should happen here? Without the design memorandum, geological investigation report, and as-built drawings, this question cannot be answered. Every safety review and forensic investigation begins from the technical documentation. Dams that have lost their design records are assessed in the dark — and NDSA inspectors have no tolerance for this gap.
What the law requires
  • Design memorandum — original design basis, hydraulic and structural calculations
  • As-built drawings — foundation levels, section dimensions, gate dimensions, instrumentation locations
  • Geological investigation reports — foundation characterisation, borehole logs
  • Construction quality control records — compaction tests, concrete mix records, material tests
  • Records of all major repairs, alterations, and enlargements — Section 2(k)
  • All inspection and CDSE reports retained

14
DHARMA Portal — Registration & Compliance
NDSA Regulations · Specified Dams Only · Digital Compliance
✓ Compliant
~ Partial
✗ Non-compliant
▼
Why this clause exists
DHARMA (Dam Health and Rehabilitation Monitoring Application) is NDSA's digital platform for tracking the safety status of all specified dams in India. It converts compliance from a paper-based, office-bound system — where records are lost in transfers or inaccessible to regulators — into a real-time national database. DHARMA is mandatory only for specified dams. Non-registration or incomplete uploads are directly visible to NDSA as compliance failures.
What the law requires
  • Specified dam registered on DHARMA — basic dam data, owner details, classification
  • Inspection questionnaire current — pre and post-monsoon data uploaded within prescribed timelines
  • All CDSE reports uploaded within 30 days of completion — NDSA Regulation 14/2022
  • Hazard classification, RA results, and EAP status recorded on DHARMA
  • DSU/DSO details recorded; Section 2(g) incidents reported through DHARMA
